Many offices and practices have been very successful at building or supporting Reach and Relevance activities across their own communities. In April 2017, we found out exactly how they executed these activities, key success factors, KPIs to measure success, inspirational examples, tips and helpful documents, and key contacts for others who might want to scale up similar Reach and Relevance pursuits.

Executive roundtables

Executive roundtables can be a great way to engage deeply with a small group of senior people. They are usually small, and exclusive group gatherings of 10-30 high-profile clients, potential clients and influencers – to discuss the latest insights on a key topic and brainstorm together on solutions.

Design Tips:
  • Establish a community of peers. Convene a group of influencers (10 - 30 people) for dinner and exchange informally on a topic of importance to them and to McKinsey
  • Short presentation & facilitator’s role. Share key facts in a short introduction to shape the debate. Offer key decision makers a safe opportunity to express their views in a critical exclusive circle
  • Position McKinsey as an institution you need to be connected to because of: its network and ability to get together senior executives from leading companies and its gathering of viewpoints that people won’t get otherwise
Key Success Factors:
  • Test the idea of having a McKinsey event. In certain cases co-hosting at existing industry forums could allow us to gain access to top clients
  • Secure at least one senior partner who is willing to invest significant time ‘every week’ to personally build the roundtable, especially in the first year
  • Define a critical topic for your community and make sure you invite people who have a common appetite / interest
  • Build an invitee list of clients, but go beyond that as well. Are there potential clients, lost clients, and influencers that your cell would like to involve in this meeting? Include local priority clients and determine backups
  • Test group homogeneity including role, tenure, company type, and geography. Be thoughtful about competitive conflicts
  • Limit attendees, nurturing a group of no more than 30 but not less than ten executives that sees itself as an ongoing group
  • Consider including spouses and partners at events (only for the social part of events). It can deepen an executive’s commitment to an event. It also underscores your interest in engaging him or her as a whole person
  • Start booking calendars 6 months in advance and send the ‘Save the Date’ at least three months in advance to make sure you have the right people in the room. Send an invitation reminder
  • Secure preparation meetings with key participants to shape the content around participant needs and make them feel invested in the outcome. Include proprietary local data if you can
  • Limit content. Delegates enjoy a more relaxed agenda with more room for networking and discussions rather than presentations. Consider including a moderated conversation amongst attendees instead of a prominent speaker.
  • Limit McKinsey voice share and participants. A 4:1 client to McKinsey ratio is ideal. The meeting should be less about sharing McKinsey’s insights and more about facilitating a discussion between the group of executives.
  • Send an invitation reminder 1-2 days before, and account for a 25% attrition the day of the event
  • Resist the urge to send pre-work to attendees, however, details about the agenda and biographies of attendees are often well received and can strengthen expectations and interaction at the meeting
  • Create a confidential setting by announcing Chatham House rules in the introduction (whereby a member may take information away from the roundtable but neither the identity of the speaker nor their organization may be revealed)
  • Survey the participants to understand what they liked, what we could work on, and to measure success. Consider leaving some time at the end of the meeting to collect feedback because post-meeting surveys usually get have a low response rate
  • Leverage the discussions and explore opportunities for follow-up discussions to make the most of the event and build long-term relationships with attendees
