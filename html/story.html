Denmark Knowledge Advisory Board: Executive roundtables

Issue #1, Monday, March 20th, 2017


The Denmark office found that CEOs wanted to discuss how ‘global disruptive forces’ could impact and help improve their companies, as well as Danish society at large.

On 21 November 2016, at the inaugural meeting of the Denmark Knowledge Advisory Board, Dominic Barton and five of our McKinsey knowledge leaders held a closed- door discussion on global disruptive forces with thirteen of Denmark’s most respected CEOs. The group discussed the impact automation could have on the Danish economy and the future of work, how companies today are embracing digital transformations and advanced analytics, what it would take for Danish companies to win in emerging markets, a new wave of consumers (and competitors) to keep an eye on as we move towards 2030, and finally how successful organizations dynamically shift resources to meet strategic goals under disruptive external conditions.

The knowledge advisory board meeting was received extremely well. 14 of 18 influencers accepted the meeting invitation. It has created a bi-annual platform to continue follow up discussions with CEOs on how we can serve them better (for instance one CEO thought we should focus on disruptions as a ‘value driver’ and an opportunity rather than a ‘value disrupter’). It has also sparked a group commitment to inform policy-makers about the role corporate citizens can play to improve the competitiveness and success of Danish businesses and Denmark’s broader society (the group has decided to create a broader event with 100 leaders who will engage policy-makers on an agenda. Sven Smit led a similar effort in the Netherlands, and so far it has helped over 2,000 schools, 1,000 companies and 30,000 individuals with impact).

Events like this show our clients that we are more than "just" a consulting firm that bids competitively for projects, as they pop up. We become convenors of a "club" in the best connotations of the word (“Club” – a group of people, organizations, or nations having something in common)

– Peter Dahlström, Senior Partner, London

Key success factors:
  • Invite your clients, but go beyond that as well. Are there influencers, like the Minister of Finance perhaps, that you would like to involve in this meeting? Are there former clients or potential companies that you can engage?
  • Start booking calendars 6 months in advance to make sure you have the right people in the room. Our intention was to make this a long-term investment, and we made this clear by positioning the first meeting as a pilot for a series of annual meetings
  • Hold meeting content discussions with key attendants, refining the agenda over time and making them feel invested in the outcome
  • Limit McKinsey voice share—the meeting should be less about sharing McKinsey’s insights and more about facilitating a discussion between the group of CEOs
  • Create a confidential and exclusive setting—we held the meeting in a private conference room with the organizing partner Stephen Creasy himself stepping out of the room
